Our Journey

The prophetic return of the children of Israel to the Promised Land after centuries in captivity, precisely as foretold by the prophets.

The Divine Vision (1966)

In February 1966, as H.E. Ben Ammi lay deeply troubled by the predicament of his people in America, the Angel Gabriel appeared with a divine message that would change history:

"It is time to start the journey back to the promised land and to establish the long awaited kingdom of God that had been prophesied by Daniel the prophet in the second chapter and the 44th verse. And the year of the Exodus will be 1967."

This 45-second vision contained the blueprint for liberation—a divine mandate to return to the Holy Land and establish Yah's Kingdom on earth. It came at a pivotal moment when social upheaval was sweeping across America, with riots erupting in cities nationwide.

When a massive snowstorm blanketed Chicago in January 1967, H.E. Ben Ammi saw it as divine confirmation: "As I walked out of my home on that morning, it appeared as if the entire world stood still, for all of the great awesome power of the Gentiles had been brought to naught by an accumulation of little snowflakes."

The Exodus Begins (1967)

In April 1967, approximately 400 Hebrew Israelites departed America for Liberia, marking the beginning of their prophetic exodus. This departure occurred almost exactly 400 years after the first Africans were brought to American shores in 1619, fulfilling the biblical timeline of captivity.

Led by H.E. Ben Ammi, these pioneers left behind comfort, possessions, and familiarity to follow divine guidance. They sold homes, quit jobs, and said goodbye to loved ones, believing wholeheartedly in the vision of returning to their ancestral homeland.

Wilderness Purification (1967-1969)

The Liberian wilderness served as a crucible for spiritual purification, testing the resolve of the Hebrew Israelites through extreme physical challenges. They faced venomous snakes, torrential monsoon rains, tropical diseases, and the difficult transition from urban American life to subsistence farming.

These hardships served a divine purpose—to burn away the "mentality of Babylon" and prepare the Saints for their ultimate destination in the Holy Land. Many who began the journey turned back, unable to endure these trials, but those who persevered emerged spiritually strengthened and unified.

Return to the Promised Land (1969-1970)

On August 19, 1969, Prince Gavriel, along with Emah Tekia and their son Benainu, became the first family to immigrate from Liberia to Israel. This historic arrival marked the beginning of the prophetic return that would bring thousands of Hebrew Israelites back to the land of their ancestors after nearly 2,000 years of exile.

The early settlers established themselves first in Arad before expanding to Dimona and Mitzpeh Ramon. Despite limited resources and an uncertain welcome, these pioneers laid the foundation for what would become a thriving community.

The Mountain of Life (1971)

On October 24, 1971, as the sun rose over the Judean hills, the Saints gathered on what would become known as Har Kayeem (Mountain of Life) in Dimona. This historic assembly marked the establishment of divine governance in the Holy Land—the first true expression of righteous leadership on earth in nearly 2,000 years.

Following the pattern established by Moses under Jethro's counsel (Exodus 18), H.E. Ben Ammi appointed twelve Princes to form the Divine Council. Each Prince received a spiritual title reflecting their divine purpose, creating the governance structure that would guide the community through the challenges ahead.

The Test of Patience (1973-1986)

For many years, the community faced significant legal challenges regarding their status in Israel. Many Saints experienced deportations, employment difficulties, and the constant pressure of building a new society in an unwelcoming environment.

Despite these obstacles, the community remained focused on establishing their Kingdom institutions—the House of Life, Kingdom School in Holiness, and various self-sustaining enterprises. They maintained their distinctive cultural practices, Genesis Nutrition, and spiritual observances despite external pressure.

Covenant Remembered (1990)

In 1990, after exactly twenty years—a biblically significant period of testing—the Israeli government granted our community members B-1 work visas, beginning the process of legal recognition. This breakthrough came through persistent diplomacy, growing recognition of our positive contributions to Israeli society, and ultimately, divine timing in fulfillment of prophecy.

Today, thousands of Saints live in Israel with permanent residency or citizenship, raising the third and fourth generations in the Kingdom way of life—living proof that Yah's word does not return void.

Holy Days & Celebrations

Sacred observances that connect us to our heritage and to Yah's divine calendar.

The Sabbath (Shabbat)

Weekly observance (sunset Friday to sunset Saturday)

The Sabbath (Shabbat) stands as the most honored of the High Holy Days, a day of spiritual communion with Yah through fasting, meditation, and sanctification. From sunset Friday to sunset Saturday, the Saints enter a sacred time set apart from worldly concerns.

Unlike the surrounding society that treats Sabbath as a feast day, we observe it with fasting from food and water, following the example of ancient prophets. During this holy time, all nourishment comes from spiritual sustenance, not physical food.

Passover (Pessach)

Commemorating ancient and modern exodus

Passover commemorates both the ancient deliverance from Egyptian bondage and our modern exodus from America in 1967. This powerful observance connects past and present, reminding us of Yah's consistent work of liberation across generations.

Our Passover celebration includes a ceremonial meal, recounting of the exodus story, and special prayers. We recognize that just as Yah passed over the houses of Israel in ancient times, He has preserved us through our modern journey from captivity to freedom.

Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur)

Most solemn observance, 24-hour complete fast

The Day of Atonement stands as our most solemn holy day, a time of deep spiritual reflection, repentance, and renewal. For a full 24 hours, we abstain completely from food and water, focusing our entire being on spiritual cleansing and reconciliation with Yah.

This sacred day includes extended prayer, confession of sins (both individual and collective), and commitment to walking more perfectly in Yah's ways. It serves as an annual spiritual reset, allowing us to enter the new year with renewed purpose and purified hearts.

Feast of Tabernacles (Sukkhote)

Commemorating wilderness experience

The Feast of Tabernacles connects us to both the ancient Israelites' wilderness journey and our own community's experience in Liberia. During this festival, we construct temporary dwellings (sukkahs) and spend time in them, remembering the transient nature of our wilderness experience.

This joyous celebration includes community gatherings, special meals, music, and pilgrimage to Jerusalem. It serves as a powerful reminder that Yah sustains His people even in the most challenging circumstances.

Kingdom Celebrations

New World Passover, Day of Appreciation

Beyond the traditional Hebrew holy days, we observe celebrations unique to our community's journey and establishment. These include New World Passover (commemorating our exodus from America), the Day of Appreciation (honoring our elders and pioneers), and National Vegan Day.

These Kingdom celebrations strengthen our identity as a community and provide regular opportunities to honor our history, express gratitude, and reaffirm our commitment to Yah's Kingdom on earth.

Preventive Health Observances

No Salt Days, Live Food Week, Sugarless Week

Our community observes regular health practices that align with our Genesis Nutrition principles. These include No Salt Days, Live Food Day/Week, and Sugarless Week, which serve both physical and spiritual purposes.

These observances help cleanse the body, reset nutritional patterns, and reinforce our commitment to prevention rather than treatment. They also serve as reminders that physical discipline contributes to spiritual clarity and receptivity.

Prince Gavriel Ha Gadol: Author Profile

"For I know the thoughts that I think toward you, saith Yah, thoughts of peace, and not of evil, to give you an expected end." —Jeremiah 29:11

Prince Gavriel Ha Gadol, known as the "Prince of Love," stands among the foundational pioneers of Yah's Kingdom on earth. His life embodies the transformation from spiritual death to resurrection that defines the African Hebrew Israelite journey.

Born George Butler on February 28, 1937, in Delhi, Louisiana during the latter years of the Great Depression, Prince Gavriel's early life reflected the experiences of many African Americans in the segregated South—close family bonds amid challenging circumstances.

On August 19, 1969, Prince Gavriel, along with Tekia and their son Benainu, became the first family to immigrate from Liberia to Israel, marking the beginning of the prophetic return to the Holy Land. This historic journey completed the pattern established by the ancient Israelites—from bondage to wilderness to promised inheritance.

Upon settling in Israel, Prince Gavriel was appointed as one of the twelve Princes of the Divine Council in 1971, receiving the spiritual title "Prince of Love." His contributions to the community included the introduction of healing herbs like fenugreek (which he named "Love Powder") and development of nutritional approaches that transformed community health.

"When I look at our community today—the Village of Peace, our healthy children and grandchildren, the international recognition we've received—I see not just our accomplishment but divine vindication. We were ex-Negroes from America's ghettos—people systematically stripped of identity, dignity, and strength. Now we stand as Hebrew Israelites firmly established in the Holy Land, unbroken despite decades of opposition. This transformation is the true miracle."

How did you know you were Hebrew Israelites?

Our identity as Hebrew Israelites was revealed through both divine revelation and scriptural study. When we examined passages like Deuteronomy 28, which describes the curses that would befall Israel for disobedience, we recognized our own historical experience of slavery, oppression, and identity loss perfectly described in these prophecies.

Deuteronomy 28:68 specifically mentions the children of Israel being taken back into Egypt (symbolizing bondage) in ships—a clear reference to the transatlantic slave trade. The scripture goes on to say they would be sold as bondmen and bondwomen with "none to buy you" (or redeem you), exactly matching our experience in chattel slavery where we were property with no redemption rights.

Our identity was not something we invented but something we recovered through divine revelation and careful study of Scripture, archaeology, and history—all of which confirmed that the descendants of the transatlantic slave trade were indeed the children of Israel in exile, precisely as prophesied.

What is Genesis Nutrition?

Genesis Nutrition is our community's approach to diet and health based on Genesis 1:29: "Behold, I have given you every herb bearing seed, which is upon the face of all the earth, and every tree, in the which is the fruit of a tree yielding seed; to you it shall be for meat."

We follow a plant-based, vegan diet that emphasizes living foods, minimal processing, and alignment with natural cycles. This preventative and regenerative approach has virtually eliminated common Western diseases within our community and produced generations of exceptionally healthy children.

Our nutrition system focuses not merely on avoiding illness but on actively promoting vibrant health, longevity, and spiritual clarity. We view food as medicine and recognize that physical purity contributes to spiritual receptivity.
