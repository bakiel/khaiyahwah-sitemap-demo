Return to the Promised Land (1969–1970)

[Image Placeholder: Early pioneers arriving in Israel or settling in Dimona/Arad]

After enduring the trials of the Liberian wilderness, the time arrived for the final leg of the exodus: the return to the Holy Land. In August 1969, the first group of pioneers, including key figures like Prince Gavriel Ha Gadol and Prince Hezkiyahoo, set foot in Israel.

This arrival marked a monumental moment—the first organized return of Hebrew Israelites to their ancestral homeland after nearly two millennia of dispersion. It was the physical manifestation of prophecies foretelling the gathering of the scattered children of Israel from the four corners of the earth.

The initial groups settled primarily in the Negev desert towns of Arad and Dimona. These locations, though challenging desert environments, were chosen for their connection to biblical history and their potential for building self-sufficient communities away from the distractions of major urban centers.

Establishing a foothold in the land required immense faith and resilience. The pioneers faced skepticism from authorities, limited resources, and the difficulties of adapting to a new culture and environment. Yet, guided by the divine vision, they began the arduous process of building homes, establishing community structures, and laying the groundwork for the Kingdom of Yah in the land promised to their forefathers.
